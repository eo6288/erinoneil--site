What your problem looks like:

This approach is applicable to discrete, linear problems of the form $\textbf{d} = \textbf{G}\textbf{m}$.


You have more parameters to estimate than data points to contrain it. Bottom line: You are in this category because your problem has a nontrivial null space. If you try the least squares (L2) route on your problem, then you will find that inverting involves evaluating the determinant of $(G^TG)$ which is zero. Or you might have equivalently noticed that your $(G^TG)$ has zero eigenvalues. Basically, you have infinitely many solutions!
