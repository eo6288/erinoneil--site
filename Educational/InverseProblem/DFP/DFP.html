DFP


What your problem looks like:

The "Davidon–Fletcher–Powell" approach is applicable to problems of the form $\textbf{d} = g(\textbf{m})$ where you are able to reasonably linearize it to $\delta \textbf{d} = \textbf{G} \delta \textbf{m}$. Ideally, you would want to use regular Newton's Method. However, computing the Hessian takes $O(n^2)$ to store and $O(n^3)$ to invert (not to mention there may be difficulties inverting). So instead, we compute $B_k \approx H_k$ iteratively. We enforce a secant equation:

$B_{k+1} s_k = y_k$
$s_k = x_{k+1} - x_k$
$y_k = \nabla f_{k+1} - \nabla f_k$

The problem with this is that $B_{k+1}$ is a matrix in a vector equation. There are actually infinitely many matrices that can satisy this! How do we chose the best one? Unlike the BFGS, the DFP makes use of the Hessian directly $H_k = (\nabla^2 f)^{-1}$ and updates:

$H_{k+1} = H_k + \frac{s_k s_k^T}{s_k^T y^k} - \frac{H_ky_k y_k^T H_k}{y_k^T H_k y_k}$

The intuition for this is that the first updating term adds curvature in the direction of the step. The second updating term subtracts away incorrect curvature from the previous step. Some downsides of this approach are that it is numerically unstable and less robust in practice.
