BFGS

Quasi-Newton methods learn about curvature from the gradients alone (curvature aware step without needing the second derivative). Inverse theory on a gradient field?

Like the conjugate gradient, the "Broyden–Fletcher–Goldfarb–Shanno" (BFGS) is considered a "blind" optimization tool, meaning, it can only feel the slope beneath its feet. Therefore, plateaus and extremely jagged surfaces are its enemy. Multiplying by the inverse Hessian can make steep canyons more bowl like. When the Hessian is super expensive to calculate, we turn to diagonal approximations (Quasi-Newton methods) to estimate its effects.

What your problem looks like:

This approach is applicable to problems of the form $\textbf{d} = g(\textbf{m})$ where you are able to reasonably linearize it to $\delta \textbf{d} = \textbf{G} \delta \textbf{m}$. Ideally, you would want to use regular Newton's Method. However, computing the Hessian takes $O(n^2)$ to store and $O(n^3)$ to invert (not to mention there may be difficulties inverting). So instead, we compute $B_k \approx H_k$ iteratively. We enforce a secant equation:

$B_{k+1} s_k = y_k$
$s_k = x_{k+1} - x_k$
$y_k = \nabla f_{k+1} - \nabla f_k$

The problem with this is that $B_{k+1}$ is a matrix in a vector equation. There are actually infinitely many matrices that can satisy this! How do we chose the best one? The BFGS builds a more stable Hessian than the DFP:

$H_{k+1} = (I - \frac{s_k y_k^T}{y^T s_k})H_k (I - \frac{y_k s_k^T}{y^T s_k}) + \frac{s_k s_k^T}{y^T s_k}$

The intuition behind this is that it projects out "bad" curvature and then adds the correct curvature. Its strength is that it can guarantee positive semi-definitness so long as $y_k^T s_k > 0$ (which I think is necessary to guarantee you step in the right direction)
